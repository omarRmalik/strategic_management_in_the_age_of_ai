# Strategic Management in the Age of Artificial Intelligence

**Omar Malik, PhD — Kettering University | Summer 2026**

---

## Overview

This repository contains the complete curriculum design for a graduate-level strategic management course that examines how artificial intelligence transforms competitive strategy, organizational capabilities, and executive decision making.

The course is built on a single premise: AI does not replace strategic management — it changes the environment in which strategic management operates. Students engage equally with the classical strategy canon (Porter, Barney, Teece, March, Grant) and contemporary AI scholarship, placing both into direct dialogue to answer one overarching question:

> **Which assumptions of classical strategic management remain valid in the age of AI, which require modification, and which should be replaced?**

---

## What Makes This Course Different

Most strategy courses teach established frameworks. Most AI courses focus on algorithms and tools. This course focuses on the *evolution of strategic management itself*.

Rather than memorizing Porter's Five Forces or VRIO, students:

- Understand why each classical theory emerged and what assumptions it makes
- Evaluate how AI reinforces, challenges, or transforms those assumptions
- Propose theoretically grounded revisions to classical frameworks
- Apply their analysis to a real organization through a semester-long consulting engagement

Students leave the course not just as consumers of strategic theory, but as contributors to its next generation.

## Course Structure

The course runs 10 weeks and follows a consistent four-phase progression:

| Phase | Weeks | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Foundations | 1–2 | Strategic thinking, AI fundamentals, competitive analysis |
| Organizational Capabilities | 3–5 | Dynamic capabilities, learning, knowledge management |
| Strategic Transformation | 6–8 | Strategic choice, governance, AI ecosystems |
| Integration | 9–10 | Synthesis, executive communication, board presentation |

## The AISE Framework

The **AI Strategy Evolution Framework (AISE)** is the organizing structure for the entire course. Every reading, laboratory, case analysis, and consulting milestone maps to one of its seven stages:

```
Environmental Change
        │
        ▼
1. Strategic Diagnosis         — What strategic problem should the organization solve?
        │
        ▼
2. Capability Assessment       — What capabilities does the organization possess?
        │
        ▼
3. AI Opportunity Identification — Where can AI create strategic value?
        │
        ▼
4. Strategic Choice            — What alternative creates the greatest long-term value?
        │
        ▼
5. Governance & Risk           — How should AI be governed responsibly?
        │
        ▼
6. Organizational Transformation — How should the organization change?
        │
        ▼
7. Continuous Learning & Renewal — How will the organization sustain competitive advantage?
```

## Weekly Scholarly Dialogue

Each week pairs a foundational strategy paper with a contemporary AI article:

| Week | Classical Strategy | Contemporary AI |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Porter (1996) — *What Is Strategy?* | Hutzschenreuter et al. (2025) — AI strategy frameworks |
| 2 | Barney (1991) — Resource-Based View | Biloslavo et al. (2024) — AI and strategic planning |
| 3 | Teece, Pisano & Shuen (1997) — Dynamic Capabilities | Recent AI & Dynamic Capabilities article |
| 4 | March (1991) — Organizational Learning | Jarrahi (2018) — Human–AI symbiosis |
| 5 | Grant (1996) — Knowledge-Based Theory | Shrestha et al. (2019) — AI decision structures |
| 6 | Prahalad & Hamel (1990) — Core Competencies | Dellermann et al. (2019) — Hybrid intelligence |
| 7 | Williamson (1979) — Transaction Cost Economics | Kellogg et al. (2020) — Algorithmic management |
| 8 | Wernerfelt (1984) — Resource-Based View origins | Rai et al. (*MIS Quarterly*) — Digital platforms |
| 9 | Teece (2007) — Dynamic capabilities microfoundations | von Krogh (2018) — AI and management theory |
| 10 | Course synthesis | Makarius et al. (2020) — Human–AI collaboration |

Students approach every paired reading with four questions: What is the theory's central contribution? What assumptions does it make? How does the AI reading challenge those assumptions? If the author were writing today, how would the theory change?

## Recurring Learning Activities

Every week includes the same five components:

**Scholarly Dialogue** — Compare a classical strategy paper with a contemporary AI article and evaluate how AI reshapes underlying assumptions.

**Theory Evolution Exercise** — A short written reflection responding to: *"If I were rewriting this theory in 2026, I would..."* Students summarize the theory, identify its assumptions, evaluate AI's impact, and propose revisions. These ten reflections accumulate into the final synthesis paper.

**AI Strategy Laboratory** — Hands-on exercises where students use approved AI tools to perform strategic analysis, then verify, critique, and evaluate AI-generated outputs using scholarly evidence. Labs align with AISE stages and produce professional consulting deliverables (industry briefs, capability maps, governance frameworks, transformation roadmaps).

**Harvard Business School Case Discussion** — Every other week, students analyze an organization confronting significant AI-related strategic challenges (*AI Wars*, *HubSpot and Motion AI*, *Osaro: Picking the Best Path*, *JPMorganChase: Leadership in the Age of GenAI*).

**Consulting Project Milestone** — Each week's milestone builds one section of the semester consulting report.

---

## Signature Learning Experience: The AI Strategy Consulting Project

Student teams serve as strategic advisors to an existing organization (manufacturing, financial services, healthcare, retail, technology, logistics, or other sector) navigating AI-driven transformation.

Weekly milestones build cumulatively toward three final deliverables:

1. **Executive Consulting Report** (30–40 pages) — organized around all seven AISE stages, including industry analysis, capability assessment, AI opportunity portfolio, strategic recommendations, governance framework, and implementation roadmap
2. **Executive Board Presentation** (25–30 minutes) — teams defend recommendations before a simulated Board of Directors and respond to questions
3. **Theory Evolution Paper** — each student's individual synthesis of their ten weekly reflections into a scholarly argument: *Toward a Theory of Strategic Management in the Age of Artificial Intelligence*

---

## AI Policy

AI is treated throughout this course as a thinking partner, not an answer generator. Students are encouraged to use AI to brainstorm, compare frameworks, summarize literature, and improve communication — but every recommendation must ultimately reflect independent professional judgment.

Students are expected to verify all AI-generated outputs against peer-reviewed sources, disclose substantive AI assistance, and critically evaluate AI claims rather than treating them as authoritative.
