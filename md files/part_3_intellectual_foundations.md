# Part III

# Intellectual Foundations

> *"The purpose of reading the classics is not to preserve old ideas, but to understand the intellectual foundations upon which new ideas are built."*

---

# Overview

This course is built upon a simple premise:

**Artificial intelligence does not replace strategic management—it transforms the context in which strategic management operates.**

Consequently, understanding strategy in the age of AI requires students to master two complementary bodies of scholarship.

The first consists of the foundational theories that have shaped strategic management over the past five decades.

The second consists of contemporary research examining how artificial intelligence changes organizations, competitive advantage, leadership, decision making, knowledge creation, and firm performance.

Rather than studying these literatures independently, students will place them into direct dialogue.

Every week asks the same overarching question:

> **How should strategic management theory evolve in the age of artificial intelligence?**

---

# Learning Philosophy

Unlike traditional strategy courses, students will not simply memorize frameworks.

Instead they will:

* understand the original theory,
* evaluate its assumptions,
* analyze contemporary AI research,
* compare competing perspectives,
* determine whether AI reinforces or challenges the theory,
* propose how the theory should evolve.

The objective is to develop scholars and executives capable of extending existing knowledge rather than simply applying established frameworks.

# The Classical Strategy Canon

The following ten papers form the intellectual foundation of modern strategic management.

Every strategy student should understand these works before graduating.

| Theme                          | Reading                                                                                                                                        | Why It Matters                                                            |
| ------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Strategic Positioning          | Porter, M. E. (1996). *What Is Strategy?* *Harvard Business Review, 74*(6), 61–78.                                                             | Defines competitive positioning and strategic trade-offs.                 |
| Resource-Based View            | Barney, J. B. (1991). Firm resources and sustained competitive advantage. *Journal of Management, 17*(1), 99–120.                              | Explains competitive advantage through strategic resources.               |
| Resource-Based View Origins    | Wernerfelt, B. (1984). A resource-based view of the firm. *Strategic Management Journal, 5*(2), 171–180.                                       | Introduces the resource-based perspective.                                |
| Dynamic Capabilities           | Teece, D. J., Pisano, G., & Shuen, A. (1997). Dynamic capabilities and strategic management. *Strategic Management Journal, 18*(7), 509–533.   | Explains adaptation in rapidly changing environments.                     |
| Dynamic Capabilities Extension | Teece, D. J. (2007). Explicating dynamic capabilities. *Strategic Management Journal, 28*(13), 1319–1350.                                      | Provides the microfoundations of strategic renewal.                       |
| Organizational Learning        | March, J. G. (1991). Exploration and exploitation in organizational learning. *Organization Science, 2*(1), 71–87.                             | Examines innovation and organizational learning.                          |
| Knowledge-Based View           | Grant, R. M. (1996). Toward a knowledge-based theory of the firm. *Strategic Management Journal, 17*(Winter Special Issue), 109–122.           | Positions knowledge as the firm's most important strategic asset.         |
| Core Competence                | Prahalad, C. K., & Hamel, G. (1990). The core competence of the corporation. *Harvard Business Review, 68*(3), 79–91.                          | Explains organizational capabilities as sources of competitive advantage. |
| Transaction Cost Economics     | Williamson, O. E. (1979). Transaction-cost economics: The governance of contractual relations. *Journal of Law and Economics, 22*(2), 233–261. | Explains organizational boundaries and governance.                        |
| Competing Perspectives         | Eisenhardt, K. M., & Martin, J. A. (2000). Dynamic capabilities: What are they? *Strategic Management Journal, 21*(10–11), 1105–1121.          | Challenges and extends the dynamic capabilities perspective.              |

# Reading Strategy

Students are expected to approach every reading using four questions.

## Question 1

**What is the central theoretical contribution?**

---

## Question 2

**What assumptions does the author make about organizations, managers, technology, or competition?**

---

## Question 3

**How does the AI reading reinforce, extend, or challenge those assumptions?**

---

## Question 4

**If the classical author were writing today, how might the theory change?**

These questions provide the intellectual foundation for class discussions, written reflections, AI laboratories, and the semester consulting project.

---

# Integrative Reading Framework

Every week's readings are organized around the following progression:

```text
Classical Theory
        ↓
Underlying Assumptions
        ↓
Contemporary AI Research
        ↓
Critical Comparison
        ↓
Managerial Implications
        ↓
Organizational Application
        ↓
Future Theory Development
```

Students move beyond understanding existing theories to evaluating how those theories should evolve in response to AI-enabled organizations.

# Intellectual Contribution of the Course

Most strategy courses teach the strategic management canon.

Most AI courses teach contemporary technologies.

This course intentionally combines both traditions through a structured scholarly dialogue.

Rather than asking students to choose between classical strategy and artificial intelligence, the curriculum demonstrates that meaningful strategic leadership requires understanding both. By critically comparing foundational theories with emerging AI research, students develop the analytical skills needed to interpret, adapt, and extend strategic management for a rapidly changing technological landscape.

The goal is not simply to prepare students for today's AI tools, but to equip them with the intellectual framework necessary to evaluate future technological developments that have yet to emerge. In doing so, the course encourages students to become active participants in the continuing evolution of strategic management as both a scholarly discipline and a managerial practice.
